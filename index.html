The Problem

Home ownership is the single largest financial investment most Americans will ever make — and yet there is no category-defining tool that helps them manage it. The closest analogs are scattered: a notes app for contractor numbers, a shoebox of receipts, a spreadsheet someone made three years ago, and a text thread with a real estate agent asking if they know a plumber.

The pain is especially acute for new homeowners aged 20–50. They've just closed the most expensive transaction of their lives and immediately face a waterfall of unknowns: who do I call when the HVAC makes noise? When was the last time anyone serviced this boiler? What was included in my home warranty? Who do I trust in this area?

Problems Homeowners Face
🔧
No maintenance record
Most homeowners have zero organized history of what's been done to their home — creating costly gaps and repeat service calls.
🔍
Finding reliable contractors is a nightmare
Angi and Yelp are noisy, untrustworthy, and pay-to-play. Word of mouth is slow. New homeowners have no network.
📋
Documents are everywhere and nowhere
Warranties, appliance manuals, closing docs — scattered across email, paper, and Google Drive folders no one can find.
💸
Home expenses are untracked
Homeowners often have no clear picture of what they're spending on their property annually — preventing smart budgeting.
The Real Estate Agent Problem (Your GTM Insight)
Direct Insight From the Field
Real estate agents on Long Island are spending entire working days calling electricians, plumbers, and contractors on behalf of clients who recently closed — because those clients have nowhere else to turn. The agent becomes an informal concierge for home services, a role they neither signed up for nor get compensated for. This is a massive, unaddressed inefficiency — and it's your direct distribution channel.
The Market Gap

Existing solutions are either too narrow (Centriq: appliances only), too complex (HomeZada), too shallow (BrightNest: tips only), or pure marketplaces with no management layer (Angi, Thumbtack). Nobody has combined the full management suite with a human concierge and a curated, trust-based service provider network. That is Homi's opportunity.

Channel 3 — Service Provider Recruitment (Supply Side)

Homi's marketplace only works if the supply side — reliable, affordable, local service providers — is actively engaged. This requires a deliberate outbound recruitment effort, separate from the homeowner acquisition strategy. The goal is not to list every contractor on Long Island. The goal is to have the best ones in the network, who are actively ready to receive and accept Homi leads.

The Two-Step Pitch
Step 1 — The Soft Open
"Hey, we have homeowners in your area who actually need work done. Are you currently available and taking on jobs in [specific area]?"

This is a low-friction opener. It asks nothing. It just establishes that there is real demand waiting.
Step 2 — The Partnership Ask
"We actually run a program where we partner with reliable providers in the area — if you're the right fit, we recommend you directly to homeowners when a relevant request comes in. No upfront cost. We only take a small percentage after we've delivered you consistent work. Interested?"

This is the value proposition: new leads, no risk, no upfront commitment.
The Hard Problem — What If They Say No?
The Best Providers May Decline
Unresolved
The strongest service providers on Long Island are often already busy. They don't need more leads — and they definitely don't want to share 5% of their revenue with a platform they've never heard of. If the best plumber in Nassau County says no, do we match homeowners to the second best? That undermines Homi's core promise.
Proposed Approach: The goal must be to make it irrational for a quality provider to say no. Tactics: (1) Start the conversation with zero ask — just send them a lead for free, no strings. Prove value before asking for anything. (2) Frame the partnership as a competitive advantage — "If you're not in our network, we're sending your neighbors' jobs to someone else." (3) Build social proof fast — when providers see other local contractors benefiting, FOMO becomes a recruiting tool. See Section 15 (Open Questions) for further resolution needed.
Provider Incentive Framework — Making "No" Irrational
🎁 Send a Free Lead First Before asking for any commission agreement, send a qualified lead for free. No contract. No ask. Just: "We thought of you for this job — here's the homeowner's info." A provider who gets one good job from Homi has a reason to stay in the network. This converts skeptics into believers.
📊 Show Them the Pipeline As Homi grows, the pitch gets stronger. "We have 500 homeowners on Long Island using Homi. In your service area, we're averaging 20 job requests a month." Real numbers make the value tangible. In early stages, be honest about where you are — providers who join early get priority placement.
🏆 Scarcity + Exclusivity Homi doesn't list every provider in a category — it recommends the best. Position it as: "We only partner with one or two providers per service category per area. You'd be our recommended electrician for [zip code]." That exclusivity makes the program feel like an honor, not a fee arrangement.

Risks & Blind Spots

This section exists precisely because most business plans don't include it. The following are real, unvarnished risks — not softened. For each, a mitigation strategy is provided. Review this section quarterly and update as conditions change.

1. Marketplace Cold Start — Chicken-and-Egg Problem
High Priority
Homi needs homeowners to make the platform valuable to service providers, and needs service providers to make the platform valuable to homeowners. At zero users, neither side has a reason to engage. This is the classic two-sided marketplace problem and has killed many well-funded companies.
Mitigation: Lead with the homeowner SaaS features first — the app provides standalone value (task tracking, documents, reminders) even with zero service provider matches. This means Homi is valuable on day one, before the marketplace is built. Service provider supply can be manually curated by the Homi team while demand is building. Solve supply through human effort; let demand build organically.
2. Service Provider Bypass — Leakage After Introduction
High Priority
After a homeowner meets a contractor through Homi, the contractor may give their personal number and the homeowner may call directly the next time — cutting Homi out of the transaction and eliminating commission revenue. This is very hard to prevent entirely.
Mitigation: Homi's value to service providers is not one job — it's a consistent pipeline of new leads. Make it clearly communicated: "You get consistent work as long as jobs flow through Homi." Additionally, build in-app booking convenience so strong that going around Homi feels like more friction, not less. Long-term: the service provider portal becomes the primary booking infrastructure, making bypass structurally difficult.
3. Free Trial Conversion Rate Risk
High Priority
The 6-month free trial is the right acquisition strategy, but it defers revenue and creates significant conversion rate dependency. If only 40–50% of trial users convert to paid, SaaS revenue projections are materially impacted. We've assumed 85% conversion — this is optimistic and untested.
Mitigation: Track engagement depth during the free trial. Users who are actively logging tasks, storing documents, and using the matching feature are far more likely to convert. Build in-app nudges at month 4 reminding users of value received. The real insurance policy is the service provider commission revenue — it doesn't depend on SaaS conversion and can offset a lower conversion rate scenario.
4. The Concierge Model Doesn't Scale Without Margin Destruction
High Priority
As detailed in Section 10, the human layer that differentiates Homi is also its biggest cost driver. Without automation, the cost per user grows linearly with user count — eventually eating all margin at scale.
Mitigation: Treat automation as a Year 2 imperative, not a nice-to-have. Document every manual process from Day 1. Build the service provider portal. Implement automated matching. Track and target: average manual minutes per request, and drive it down every quarter. The human layer should handle exception cases and high-value relationships — not routine scheduling.
5. Data Infrastructure Fragility
Medium Priority
Service provider data living in an Excel sheet is a fragility risk. Data can become stale, incorrect, or lost. More importantly, it cannot power any automated matching or quality-scoring system without proper database infrastructure.
Mitigation: Migrate Excel data to a proper database within 30 days of launch. Implement a simple data freshness check — Google Calendar connection verification helps confirm providers are still active. Build a feedback loop from completed jobs into the provider quality score. Prioritize this before it becomes a user-facing problem.
6. Data Scraping & API Terms of Service Legal Exposure
Medium Priority
Aggregating provider data from Google, Angi, Yelp, Nextdoor, and Facebook via their APIs technically violates several of these platforms' ToS when used for commercial purposes without explicit permission. This is a legal risk that could result in takedown notices, cease-and-desist letters, or data loss.
Mitigation: Get a legal review from a startup attorney on current data practices. Prioritize building proprietary relationships with service providers directly — their data then belongs to Homi by consent. The current Excel-based approach (no live API feed) reduces real-time exposure. Long term: the service provider portal makes scraped data obsolete because providers input their own data directly.
7. Single-Founder GTM Dependency
Medium Priority
Alex is currently the sole driver of every brokerage relationship, social strategy, and GTM function. If Alex is pulled to a demanding period at Ramp, or if any single brokerage relationship falls through, GTM stalls. The first brokerage partnership is a single point of failure.
Mitigation: Activate the first brokerage immediately and start the second within 60 days to avoid single-channel dependency. Document the brokerage pitch and materials so that eventually someone else can execute the outreach. Consider involving a trusted advisor from the Long Island real estate community who can open doors independently.
8. Competition from Big Tech (Google, Amazon, Apple)
Medium Priority
Google Home, Amazon Home Services, and Apple's ecosystem expansions could theoretically build what Homi is building with near-infinite resources. A well-funded national competitor doubling down on the home services category could compress Homi's window.
Mitigation: Big tech moves slowly on hyper-local, service-intensive products. The human concierge layer and regional depth are things platforms at scale structurally cannot replicate. Homi's moat is intimacy and trust — not features. Build the brand and community before any large player notices the market. Geographic focus is defensive, not just strategic.
9. Post-Onboarding Churn — "I Fixed Everything, I Don't Need This Anymore"
Medium Priority
Homeowners who join in the first year of homeownership may feel they've "figured everything out" after 12–18 months and cancel. If the product feels solved, it stops feeling essential — and $7/month is the first thing people cut.
Mitigation: Homi must deliver ongoing value, not just onboarding value. Seasonal reminders, proactive maintenance alerts, expense trend reports, and new jobs through the matching platform all create recurring reasons to open the app. The goal is to make Homi feel like a continuous membership — not a one-time resource. Track monthly active usage as a leading indicator for churn risk.
10. Reputation Risk from Bad Service Provider Match
Manageable
If Homi matches a homeowner with a contractor who does poor work, disappears mid-job, or overcharges — and Homi's brand is attached to that recommendation — it creates reputation damage and potential user loss. One bad experience at a small user count can cause disproportionate harm through word of mouth.
Mitigation: Only match providers with strong quality signals (no 1-star matches is already policy). Follow up on every completed job — build a simple in-app rating flow. Establish a "Homi guarantee" policy for early users: if a matched provider disappoints, Homi personally makes it right by finding a replacement and following up. At small scale, the human layer can absorb this. At scale, the rating system automates quality control.

Open Questions & Decisions

This section is a living list. These are the decisions that haven't been made yet and the questions that need answers before Homi can operate at full capacity. Treat this as an active to-do list for the founding team. Assign an owner and a target resolution date to each item.

1. Legal Entity Formation — LLC vs. C-Corp?
Homi is currently unincorporated. Before any commercial activity, users, or revenue — a legal entity must be formed. LLC is simpler and sufficient if you never plan to raise VC. C-Corp (Delaware) is the standard if you ever want to raise institutional capital or issue equity to employees/advisors. Given Homi is currently bootstrapped with no fundraising plans, an LLC is likely fine — but this decision should be made by a startup attorney, not assumed. Get a legal consultation within 30 days.
2. Annual Subscription Pricing
Monthly is $7. What is the annual plan price? Options: $60/yr (~$5/mo, 29% discount), $65/yr (~$5.42/mo, 23% discount), or $70/yr (~$5.83/mo, 17% discount). Lower discount = more revenue per user but lower conversion to annual. Higher discount = better retention but lower per-user revenue. Recommendation: start at $70/yr — it's still an appealing discount, and locking in annual revenue with minimal churn is worth more than the $14 difference from $84/yr.
3. Service Provider Recruitment — How Do We Make "No" Irrational?
The pitch to providers is defined (see Section 7, Channel 3), but the hard problem remains: what happens when the best provider in a category declines to partner? We cannot build Homi's quality promise on second-best providers. Three approaches to resolve this: (1) Send a free lead first with zero strings — prove value before asking for anything. (2) Use exclusivity as leverage: "We only partner with one provider per category per zip code — if you're not in, someone else is." (3) Build social proof among the provider community — when local contractors see their peers getting consistent work from Homi, FOMO closes the deal. This question must be answered before large-scale provider outreach begins. It shapes the entire supply-side strategy.
4. Brokerage Referral Fee — Unit Economics Validation
The current structure ($50 per 10 activated users = $5 CAC) needs unit economics modeling before it's formally offered to brokerages. Key questions: What counts as "activated"? (Downloaded the app? Completed onboarding? Used it for 30 days?) How is this tracked and paid out? Monthly? Quarterly? What prevents a brokerage from mass-distributing QR codes to people who download but never activate? Define "activated" precisely, build a simple tracking mechanism, and confirm the $5 CAC is financially sustainable at scale before committing to the $50 per 10 structure formally.
4. Android Build — Timeline & Feature Parity
Android must be built immediately — Long Island is not a monolithic iPhone market. Jack owns this. The question is: what is the specific timeline from today to Android TestFlight? And does Android launch simultaneously with the primary iOS launch, or does it trail? The risk of trailing: any broker-referred homeowner on Android will hit a dead end. Recommendation: target Android launch within 60 days. Communicate clearly to brokers that Android is "coming in 60 days" during Phase 1 outreach.
5. Service Provider Commission Enforcement Mechanism
How exactly does Homi collect its 5% commission? Options: (a) Homi invoices the service provider directly after every 5 jobs and the provider pays via ACH or check; (b) The service provider portal eventually handles this automatically; (c) An honor system with trust until the portal is built. At launch with manual operations, option (a) or (c) is likely. Jack's payment infrastructure background is directly relevant here — how does Homi want to formally structure payment collection before the portal exists? Define this before the first commission is earned.

The service provider data must move from Excel to a proper database before launch — not after. Jack owns this. What database? What schema? What is the migration plan and timeline? This is a technical decision, but it has direct product and operational consequences. Must be resolved in the 30-day pre-launch window.

Alex and Jack are both open to involving people they know and trust. Now is the right time to identify 2–3 advisors who can provide: (a) deep Long Island real estate network access, (b) legal or compliance guidance, (c) operational/startup scaling experience. Advisors at this stage can be compensated with small equity allocations (0.1–0.5%) and provide outsized value in opening doors. Who in your respective networks fits these profiles? This is worth a deliberate conversation between Alex and Jack.
8. Defining "Activated User" for Free Trial Tracking
For both GTM purposes (brokerage referral payouts) and internal metric tracking, "activated user" needs a clear definition. Options: (a) App downloaded; (b) Account created; (c) Onboarding completed (home details entered); (d) First feature used (task created, document uploaded, or matching request submitted). Recommendation: define activation as completing onboarding + using at least one feature within 7 days. This ensures activation reflects genuine engagement, not just a download.
9. Insurance & Liability — Service Provider Matches
If Homi recommends a contractor who causes property damage, is Homi liable? This depends heavily on how the relationship is structured and what language is in the Terms of Service. Platforms like Angi use careful ToS language to disclaim liability. Homi must consult a startup attorney on this before any service provider matches are made commercially. The ToS and user agreement must be drafted with this risk in mind.
10. When Does Alex Go Full-Time on Homi?
Alex is currently leading a new business team at Ramp while building Homi. This split focus is sustainable at the zero-user stage, but at some inflection point — likely between 500–1,000 users or at first meaningful revenue — full-time dedication becomes necessary to maintain the quality and speed of GTM execution. Jack may already be full or close to full-time given the build. This is not a question to avoid — it's one of the most important founder decisions that will determine how fast Homi scales. Identify the trigger conditions (user count, MRR, or brokerage pipeline size) that would prompt the full-time transition.
11. Commission Transparency — Do Homeowners Know Homi Takes a Cut?
When Homi matches a homeowner with a contractor and takes 5% of the invoice, the homeowner is currently not informed this is happening. This is an open action item. While this does not directly affect the homeowner's cost or experience — the contractor absorbs the fee — it is worth addressing proactively in the Terms of Service and potentially in the product UI. The question to answer: how and where do we disclose this? Options: (1) Bury it in ToS only — legally sufficient but not transparent; (2) State it plainly in the app onboarding — "Homi earns a small fee from service providers when jobs are completed. This never affects your price."; (3) Use it as a trust signal — "We earn when you get great service. That's how we stay aligned." Option 3 turns a potential concern into a brand strength. Decide before launch and bake it into ToS and onboarding copy.
12. Pricing — Is $7/Month Leaving Serious Money on the Table?
$7/month was chosen without formal pricing research or user testing. Comparable competitors range from $10 to $40/month. Homi's value proposition — all-in-one management + human concierge + AI + local service matching — is materially stronger than most of those competitors. The difference between $7 and $15/month is $96/year per user. At 7,000 users that is $672,000 in annual revenue. Before launch, do the following: (1) Run a basic willingness-to-pay survey with 20–30 Long Island homeowners — ask what they'd pay for this specific value prop. (2) Research HomeZada ($9.99–$19.99), Centriq (free + premium), and others. (3) Consider a tiered model: $7/mo basic (app only) vs. $15/mo premium (app + full concierge access). Pricing is one of the highest-leverage decisions a company makes in year 1 — don't leave it at a gut number without testing.
13. Document Storage Security — What Must Be In Place Before a Single File Is Uploaded?
Required before launch: (1) Encryption at rest (AES-256) for all stored files — mortgage docs, insurance policies, warranties, and closing paperwork are highly sensitive. (2) Encryption in transit (TLS/HTTPS) on all data transmission. (3) Role-based access control — only the account holder and explicitly invited household members can access household documents. (4) Privacy Policy and Terms of Service drafted by a startup attorney that clearly states: what data is stored, how it is protected, what happens in a breach, and that Homi is not liable for user-uploaded document content. (5) Comply with the New York SHIELD Act — which requires businesses handling NY resident data to implement reasonable safeguards.

Incident response plan (if a breach occurs): (1) Detect and isolate affected systems within 24 hours. (2) Notify all affected users within 72 hours as required by law. (3) Engage a cybersecurity firm immediately. (4) Report to relevant authorities if required by SHIELD Act or CCPA. (5) Issue a public statement and user communication within 5 business days. (6) Post-incident audit and remediation report. Jack owns the technical implementation. A startup attorney must review the legal posture before document storage is promoted as a feature to users.
